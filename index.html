Introduction

Word games and puzzles are important in psychology for four major reasons: they are widely enjoyed by people and can provide large sample sizes for studies; they can reflect or represent specific cognitive constructs; they can be used to gamify testing situations that may otherwise be tedious; and they can be used to practice and improve different dimensions of verbal fluency and cognitive reserve. Word games vary in structure, which is important because some of these differences appear to be associated with the types of cognitive functions that correlate with puzzle proficiency. Studies on older word games, such as crosswords, focus heavily on human cognition, with explorations of how their relationship to cognitive reserves may be used to combat cognitive decline and dementia. Wordle, published in 2021 (Tracy, 2022), has recently been introduced to the academic literature in psychology and machine learning. These new studies tend to have a different focus, exploring how the game functions and optimized play for both machines and humans. We wish to explore this gap in the research using the available academic resources on Wordle.

Cognitive Domains and Puzzle Structure

Crossword puzzles are a very commonly researched form of word games, but results from a study by Hambrick et al. (1999) on correlations between crossword proficiency and various cognitive domains seem to indicate an unexpected lack of connection with abstract and analytical reasoning abilities. Hambrick et al. conducted four correlational studies measuring scores on various cognitive tests in areas such as general knowledge, reasoning, and vocabulary against their crossword proficiency construct, defined as the number of target words identified out of a 54–66 word puzzle within five minutes. The correlations they found with puzzle proficiency were strong for general knowledge, moderate for word retrieval and transformation performance, and not present for reasoning abilities. One interpretation they offered was that “most clues in crossword puzzles are straightforward factual references that require only knowledge to solve” (Hambrick et al., 1999, p. 155), and that these findings regarding reasoning may be different for other types of word puzzles.

Friedlander and Fine (2020) indeed found that solvers with high proficiency in British-style cryptic crosswords, wherein the clues are themselves word puzzles, also showed exceptionally high scores on a fluid reasoning test compared to the general population. They explain that some word games are quasi-algebraic, meaning that they require logic, hypothesis testing, and working memory manipulation. This construct of the quasi-algebraic puzzle could explain the difference in fluid reasoning involvement between cryptic crosswords and American-style crosswords.

Murphy et al. (2016), through their intervention study of semantic/thematic and structural word tasks on different types of verbal fluency, give us a framework with which to understand different kinds of word games and their potential to reflect certain cognitive constructs. Verbal fluency requires an ability referred to as clustering — recalling words within a specific category, moving both within and between clusters, and hierarchically organizing information. Verbal fluency can be semantic (SVF) when the categories are based on meaning, or phonemic (PVF) when they are based on letter cues. Murphy et al. elected to use crossword puzzles as their basis because their clues could be both semantic/thematic or structural. They found a significant improvement with a large effect size on PVF performance for the structural group, with no significant interaction for SVF. Overall, these findings suggest that different types of word puzzles can improve performance in different cognitive domains. We suggest that the use of Wordle, which we argue is a purely structural and phonemic type of word puzzle, may be ideal for further exploration.

Wordle and Fluid Reasoning

The sole resource we located on the cognitive functions underlying Wordle performance was a Stage 1 Registered Report by Achaa-Amankwaa et al. (2025). These authors hypothesize that Wordle is a reflection of crystallized intelligence, retrieval ability, and — what in our opinion separates this from crosswords as a potentially ideal game-based cognitive assessment — fluid reasoning. They argue that because Wordle provides real-time feedback, performance depends heavily on strategy efficiency: how well a player uses guesses to eliminate possibilities (maximize entropy reduction) rather than guessing randomly (Achaa-Amankwaa et al., 2025, pp. 14–15).

A study on goal gradients by Dixon et al. (2024) highlights an important structural difference between Wordles and crosswords: Wordle provides immediate, visual, and easily interpretable feedback after each guess in the form of colour-coded tiles. This feedback creates a goal gradient dynamic, where players are emotionally driven to pursue approach outcomes (green and yellow squares) because they boost positive affect and motivation. However, this “greedy” emotional strategy is often mathematically wrong. Dixon et al. showed that humans respond to recognized visual progress rather than to objective mathematical progress — a key insight informing our experimental manipulation.

Anderson and Meyer (2022) demonstrated that an optimal Wordle strategy using maximum green letter probabilities and reinforcement learning could reduce turn rates significantly. Their analysis revealed that a mathematically optimized path through the limited problem-solving space — one that does not require complex calculations beyond human capacity — was achievable. Evans and Turrini (2023) further support the viability of strategic guidance in sequential games, demonstrating that exploiting positional similarity can significantly improve decision-making in games with limited action spaces.

Our goal is to design a preliminary test on the potential of Wordle as a structural puzzle measuring fluid reasoning ability. If logical, strategic guidance increases Wordle turn-rate as we hypothesize, while simultaneously improving solve success, this would suggest that Wordle is more similar to cryptic crosswords than simple crosswords in that it is a quasi-algebraic game likely to require fluid reasoning. While this is not a definitive conclusion, it may provide a jumping-off point for future research on Wordle’s efficacy for cognitive testing and intervention.

Methodology

Design

This study employed a quasi-experimental within-groups design. It is considered quasi-experimental because participants were not randomly assigned to conditions; rather, all participants completed both conditions in a fixed order by design. The independent variable was strategic guidance condition (non-guided vs. guided). The dependent variable was participants’ performance on the Wordle tasks, operationalized primarily as turn-rate (the number of guesses required per puzzle) and secondarily as solve success (whether the target word was correctly identified within six guesses).

Participants

We anticipated between 20 and 25 participants in this study. The group studied consisted of psychology students taking the course PSYC 2400 Research Methods in Psychology at the Langley campus of Kwantlen Polytechnic University. All participants were at least 18 years of age and were selected through convenience sampling. A final sample of n = 20 was retained after applying operational definitions (see Data Analysis below).

Materials

Participants were expected to use an electronic device — either their phone or laptop — and were provided a testing booklet (Appendix A) to fill out and complete the tasks. Those without a device available were provided one. The testing booklet consisted of four QR codes, four Wordle tables, and four question sheets. The six secret words selected for the study were drawn from 300 daily Wordles released by the New York Times before February 1st, 2026: Wordle #1501 (OMEGA), #1625 (RATTY), #1525 (PITCH), #1389 (SPARE), #1430 (MUGGY), and #1442 (HABIT), attached in Appendix C. Strategic guidance materials are attached in Appendix B (Wordle instruction sheet) and Appendix D (strategic guidance instruction sheet).

Procedure

The order of events began with participants reading and signing informed consent forms. Once forms were collected, researchers explained the task to all participants, including a verbal explanation of what Wordle is and how the experiment would proceed. All participants were instructed to turn off any settings they may have previously changed — specifically the “hard mode” feature — to ensure all participants worked in the same environment. To reduce practice effects and beginners’ luck, all participants completed the New York Times daily Wordle on March 11th, 2026 (that day’s word: TEDDY) as a mandatory warm-up before beginning any study tasks.

Participants were then provided their questionnaire booklet. For the first two Wordles (W1 and W2), participants were instructed only to complete their assigned tasks and fill out the corresponding questionnaire pages — no strategic guidance was provided in this control condition. Participants were not permitted to share their answers with one another to control for confounds. Through random assignment, each participant received four of the six available words in a randomized order. The randomization of word order served to reduce order effects that may arise from receiving easy words before hard words and vice versa.

Once participants had completed W1 and W2, the independent variable was introduced. Participants were permitted to collect a strategic guidance sheet from the front of the room (Appendix D) and also received a verbal explanation of the guidance to reduce information overload. The guidance instructed participants to: (1) start with high-information words from a statistically optimized list in order (LOOIE → SAURY → CHANT → BIDED → PRIMP); (2) if only yellow letters are revealed, temporarily ignore them and continue down the list to exclude more letters; and (3) once a green letter appears, abandon the list and begin solving. After receiving guidance, participants completed W3 and W4. After all four sessions, questionnaires were collected and a debrief form was distributed to all participants.

Measures

A fixed-choice questionnaire (Appendix A) was administered after each Wordle session, capturing: whether the Wordle was successfully completed (yes/no); the number of guesses taken (1–6); perceived difficulty (1 = Very Easy to 5 = Very Difficult); natural strategy questions for W2 (rated on a 1–5 Likert scale); and for W3 and W4, guidance compliance (1–4 scale) and perceived usefulness of the guidance (1–5 scale). Participant start and end times were recorded manually on each questionnaire sheet.

Controls for Threats to Validity

Several steps were taken to address threats to internal validity. Order effects were reduced by randomizing word assignment and session order across participants. Practice effects were mitigated by randomizing word assignment and order, and by administering the daily Wordle as a mandatory warm-up so that any improvement from familiarity with the interface would manifest before study tasks began. Regression to the mean was addressed by including the warm-up Wordle to bring participants closer to their average performance before data collection commenced. Beginner’s luck was similarly reduced by the warm-up task.

Data Analysis

Two operational definitions were applied before analysis to ensure data quality. OD1 (guidance compliance) retained only participants who used LOOIE as their first guess in both W3 and W4, reducing the sample from n = 23 to n = 20 (P2, P11, and P19 excluded). OD2 (correct answer scoring) coded a Wordle as solved only if the participant’s final guess exactly matched the secret word, overriding any self-reported solve status contradicted by the raw guess record. The primary statistical analysis was a series of one-sided paired-samples t-tests comparing turn-rate between non-guided and guided sessions. Effect sizes were computed using Cohen’s d. Secondary analyses included point-biserial correlations and independent-samples t-tests for questionnaire data. Performance was also compared against New York Times WordleBot global averages from a population of approximately 400,000 players.

Results

01

Statistical Analysis

The primary statistical analysis used in this study was a series of one-sided paired-samples t-tests. Because this was a within-groups design — all participants completed both the non-guided (control) and guided (experimental) conditions — paired t-tests were appropriate for directly comparing each participant's performance across conditions while controlling for individual differences. A one-sided test was specified because our hypothesis was directional: we predicted that the guided condition would produce more turns (higher turn-rate) than the non-guided condition, as the prescribed strategy sequence (LOOIE → SAURY → CHANT → BIDED → PRIMP) was hypothesized to restructure the problem-solving process rather than shortcut it. Effect sizes were calculated using Cohen's d to assess practical significance alongside statistical significance.

Methodology & Hypothesis

This study employed a quasi-experimental within-groups design. Twenty psychology students at Kwantlen Polytechnic University (PSYC 2400, Langley campus) completed four Wordle puzzles in sequence: two in the non-guided condition (W1 and W2, free strategy) followed by two in the guided condition (W3 and W4, prescribed starter sequence). It is considered quasi-experimental because participants were not randomly assigned to conditions; rather, all participants completed both conditions in a fixed order by design. The independent variable was strategic guidance condition; the dependent variable was turn-rate and solve success. To contextualize performance, study results were compared against New York Times WordleBot global averages drawn from a population of approximately 400,000 players in the most recent available year. The hypothesis was: if logical, strategic guidance is applied, Wordle turn-rate will increase relative to the non-guided condition. To reduce practice effects and beginners’ luck, all participants completed the New York Times daily Wordle on March 11th, 2026 — that day’s word being TEDDY — as a mandatory warm-up before beginning any study tasks. Word assignments across the four study sessions were also randomized across participants to reduce order effects.

From Raw Data to Analyzable Data

Raw data were collected on paper questionnaire booklets and transcribed into a master spreadsheet. Two operational definitions were applied before analysis. OD1 (guidance compliance) retained only participants who used LOOIE as their first guess in both W3 and W4, reducing the sample from n = 23 to n = 20. Three participants were excluded: P2 opened W4 with YOLUS, P11 reversed the sequence order, and P19’s notes explicitly stated non-compliance. OD2 (correct answer scoring) coded a Wordle as solved only if the participant’s final guess exactly matched the secret word — overriding any self-reported solve status contradicted by the raw record. Seven corrections were made in total; the most consequential was P17, who self-reported solving both W3 and W4 but whose final guesses (GOALS, CRAMP) did not match the target words (RATTY, HABIT).

Natural Strategy (W2)

To establish whether participants were already using strategic approaches before receiving guidance, three strategy questions were included in the W2 questionnaire. Participants rated agreement with each statement on a 1–5 scale. Relying on colour feedback from previous guesses was the dominant natural strategy (M = 3.95; 80% agreed), considerably above the neutral midpoint (Figure 10). Minimizing letter overlap between guesses (M = 3.05) and starting with vowel-rich or common-letter words (M = 3.20) were more evenly spread near neutral. Figure 11 below shows the full response distributions: colour feedback had a strong right-skew (16 of 20 agreed or strongly agreed), while the other two strategies attracted substantially more disagreement — confirming that feedback-driven iteration was the primary natural approach before any guided instruction was introduced.

Perceived usefulness showed a directional but non-significant association with guess count (r = −.281, p = .079), suggesting participants who found the guidance more helpful used slightly fewer guesses on average, though this did not reach statistical significance. Together, the questionnaire findings indicate that the benefit of guidance operated primarily through whether a puzzle was ultimately solved rather than through fine-grained turn efficiency within individual attempts.

Strategic Guidance Instruction Sheet

Optimal Wordle Strategy: A 3-Step Guide

Step 1 — The High-Information Start

Begin your game by using words from the following statistically optimized list in order. Always type “LOOIE” for your very first guess. Your primary goal is to reveal a green letter.

1.LOOIE
2.SAURY
3.CHANT
4.BIDED
5.PRIMP
Step 2 — If Only Yellow(s), Exclude

If your guess shows only yellow letters (zero green letters), you must temporarily ignore them. Do not try to rearrange your yellow letters into a new word. Instead, guess the next word on the list (e.g., “SAURY”). Continue moving down the list if you continue to only receive yellow letters.

Step 3 — If Green, Solve

As soon as you reveal at least one green letter (even on your first guess, and even if you have no yellows), completely abandon the list above and start solving using all accumulated information.
