What the Wordle?:

The effect of strategic guidance on turn rates in phonemic word puzzles

Nihal O’Keefe, Darcy Marcus, Brielle Munz, and Rae Kalkett

Department of Psychology, Kwantlen Polytechnic University

PSYC 2400: Research Methods in Psychology

Supervisor: Dr. Richard Le Grand

Ethics ID: KPU REB #2024-36

April 17th, 2026

Abstract

The recency of Wordle’s publication as a language game means there is little research on its cognitive properties. Previous research suggests that different types of language games require proficiency in varied cognitive skills. The purpose of this study was to establish a precedent as to whether Wordle could serve as a useful measurement of fluid reasoning. This was a repeated measures experiment using an initial sample of 23 psychology research methods students, with 20 retained after applying operational definitions, who completed two Wordles without strategic guidance and two with, followed by a questionnaire. Turns taken, time taken, participant strategy, and perceptions of guidance usefulness were measured via survey. A pre-established optimal human strategy, which was provided to participants in the form of written and visual guidance, served as a proxy for fluid reasoning. We found that strategic guidance significantly increased turn rate as hypothesized, and that compliance with the prescribed strategy significantly predicted solve success (rpb = .436, p = .005), though guidance did not reduce completion time. Participants reported mixed perceptions on the usefulness of the guidance. These results suggest that while Wordle performance may be restructured by strategic guidance — improving solve accuracy at the cost of efficiency — the cognitive demands of applying an externally provided strategy may offset its benefits in the short term. Alternatively, addressing the methodological concerns of our study in future replications may yield different results. Future research may find usefulness in Wordle as a measure of other cognitive abilities, such as general knowledge or vocabulary.

Introduction

Word games and puzzles are important in psychology for four major reasons: they are widely enjoyed by people and can provide large sample sizes for studies; they can reflect or represent specific cognitive constructs; they can be used to gamify testing situations that may otherwise be tedious; and they can be used to practice and improve different dimensions of verbal fluency and cognitive reserve. Word games vary in structure, which is important because some of these differences appear to be associated with the types of cognitive functions that correlate with puzzle proficiency. Studies on older word games, such as crosswords, focus heavily on human cognition, with explorations of how their relationship to cognitive reserves may be used to combat cognitive decline and dementia. Wordle, published in 2021 (Tracy, 2022), has recently been introduced to the academic literature in psychology and machine learning. These new studies tend to have a different focus, exploring how the game functions and optimized play for both machines and humans. We wish to explore this gap in the research using the available academic resources on Wordle.

Cognitive Domains and Puzzle Structure

Crossword puzzles are a very commonly researched form of word games, but results from a study by Hambrick et al. (1999) on correlations between crossword proficiency and various cognitive domains seem to indicate an unexpected lack of connection with abstract and analytical reasoning abilities. Hambrick et al. conducted four correlational studies measuring scores on various cognitive tests in areas such as general knowledge, reasoning, and vocabulary against their crossword proficiency construct, defined as the number of target words identified out of a 54–66 word puzzle within five minutes. The correlations they found with puzzle proficiency were strong for general knowledge, moderate for word retrieval and transformation performance, and not present for reasoning abilities. One interpretation they offered was that “most clues in crossword puzzles are straightforward factual references that require only knowledge to solve” (Hambrick et al., 1999, p. 155), and that these findings regarding reasoning may be different for other types of word puzzles.

Friedlander and Fine (2020) indeed found that solvers with high proficiency in British-style cryptic crosswords, wherein the clues are themselves word puzzles, also showed exceptionally high scores on a fluid reasoning test compared to the general population. They explain that some word games are quasi-algebraic, meaning that they require logic, hypothesis testing, and working memory manipulation. This construct of the quasi-algebraic puzzle could explain the difference in fluid reasoning involvement between cryptic crosswords and American-style crosswords.

Murphy et al. (2016), through their intervention study of semantic/thematic and structural word tasks on different types of verbal fluency, give us a framework with which to understand different kinds of word games and their potential to reflect certain cognitive constructs. Verbal fluency requires an ability referred to as clustering — recalling words within a specific category, moving both within and between clusters, and hierarchically organizing information. Verbal fluency can be semantic (SVF) when the categories are based on meaning, or phonemic (PVF) when they are based on letter cues. Murphy et al. elected to use crossword puzzles as their basis because their clues could be both semantic/thematic or structural. They found a significant improvement with a large effect size on PVF performance for the structural group, with no significant interaction for SVF. Overall, these findings suggest that different types of word puzzles can improve performance in different cognitive domains. We suggest that the use of Wordle, which we argue is a purely structural and phonemic type of word puzzle, may be ideal for further exploration.

Wordle and Fluid Reasoning

The sole resource we located on the cognitive functions underlying Wordle performance was a Stage 1 Registered Report by Achaa-Amankwaa et al. (2025). These authors hypothesize that Wordle is a reflection of crystallized intelligence, retrieval ability, and — what in our opinion separates this from crosswords as a potentially ideal game-based cognitive assessment — fluid reasoning. They argue that because Wordle provides real-time feedback, performance depends heavily on strategy efficiency: how well a player uses guesses to eliminate possibilities (maximize entropy reduction) rather than guessing randomly (Achaa-Amankwaa et al., 2025, pp. 14–15).

A study on goal gradients by Dixon et al. (2024) highlights an important structural difference between Wordles and crosswords: Wordle provides immediate, visual, and easily interpretable feedback after each guess in the form of colour-coded tiles. This feedback creates a goal gradient dynamic, where players are emotionally driven to pursue approach outcomes (green and yellow squares) because they boost positive affect and motivation. However, this “greedy” emotional strategy is often mathematically wrong. Dixon et al. showed that humans respond to recognized visual progress rather than to objective mathematical progress — a key insight informing our experimental manipulation.

Anderson and Meyer (2022) demonstrated that an optimal Wordle strategy using maximum green letter probabilities and reinforcement learning could reduce turn rates significantly. Their analysis revealed that a mathematically optimized path through the limited problem-solving space — one that does not require complex calculations beyond human capacity — was achievable. Evans and Turrini (2023) further support the viability of strategic guidance in sequential games, demonstrating that exploiting positional similarity can significantly improve decision-making in games with limited action spaces.

Our goal is to design a preliminary test on the potential of Wordle as a structural puzzle measuring fluid reasoning ability. If logical, strategic guidance increases Wordle turn-rate as we hypothesize, while simultaneously improving solve success, this would suggest that Wordle is more similar to cryptic crosswords than simple crosswords in that it is a quasi-algebraic game likely to require fluid reasoning. While this is not a definitive conclusion, it may provide a jumping-off point for future research on Wordle’s efficacy for cognitive testing and intervention.

Methodology

Design

This study employed a quasi-experimental within-groups design. It is considered quasi-experimental because participants were not randomly assigned to conditions; rather, all participants completed both conditions in a fixed order by design. The independent variable was strategic guidance condition (non-guided vs. guided). The dependent variable was participants’ performance on the Wordle tasks, operationalized primarily as turn-rate (the number of guesses required per puzzle) and secondarily as solve success (whether the target word was correctly identified within six guesses).

Participants

We anticipated between 20 and 25 participants in this study. The group studied consisted of psychology students taking the course PSYC 2400 Research Methods in Psychology at the Langley campus of Kwantlen Polytechnic University. All participants were at least 18 years of age and were selected through convenience sampling. A final sample of n = 20 was retained after applying operational definitions (see Data Analysis below).

Materials

Participants were expected to use an electronic device — either their phone or laptop — and were provided a testing booklet (Appendix A) to fill out and complete the tasks. Those without a device available were provided one. The testing booklet consisted of four QR codes, four Wordle tables, and four question sheets. The six secret words selected for the study were drawn from 300 daily Wordles released by the New York Times before February 1st, 2026: Wordle #1501 (OMEGA), #1625 (RATTY), #1525 (PITCH), #1389 (SPARE), #1430 (MUGGY), and #1442 (HABIT), attached in Appendix C. Strategic guidance materials are attached in Appendix B (Wordle instruction sheet) and Appendix D (strategic guidance instruction sheet).

Procedure

The order of events began with participants reading and signing informed consent forms. Once forms were collected, researchers explained the task to all participants, including a verbal explanation of what Wordle is and how the experiment would proceed. All participants were instructed to turn off any settings they may have previously changed — specifically the “hard mode” feature — to ensure all participants worked in the same environment. To reduce practice effects and beginners’ luck, all participants completed the New York Times daily Wordle on March 11th, 2026 (that day’s word: TEDDY) as a mandatory warm-up before beginning any study tasks.

Participants were then provided their questionnaire booklet. For the first two Wordles (W1 and W2), participants were instructed only to complete their assigned tasks and fill out the corresponding questionnaire pages — no strategic guidance was provided in this control condition. Participants were not permitted to share their answers with one another to control for confounds. Through random assignment, each participant received four of the six available words in a randomized order. The randomization of word order served to reduce order effects that may arise from receiving easy words before hard words and vice versa.

Once participants had completed W1 and W2, the independent variable was introduced. Participants were permitted to collect a strategic guidance sheet from the front of the room (Appendix D) and also received a verbal explanation of the guidance to reduce information overload. The guidance instructed participants to: (1) start with high-information words from a statistically optimized list in order (LOOIE → SAURY → CHANT → BIDED → PRIMP); (2) if only yellow letters are revealed, temporarily ignore them and continue down the list to exclude more letters; and (3) once a green letter appears, abandon the list and begin solving. After receiving guidance, participants completed W3 and W4. After all four sessions, questionnaires were collected and a debrief form was distributed to all participants.

Measures

A fixed-choice questionnaire (Appendix A) was administered after each Wordle session, capturing: whether the Wordle was successfully completed (yes/no); the number of guesses taken (1–6); perceived difficulty (1 = Very Easy to 5 = Very Difficult); natural strategy questions for W2 (rated on a 1–5 Likert scale); and for W3 and W4, guidance compliance (1–4 scale) and perceived usefulness of the guidance (1–5 scale). Participant start and end times were recorded manually on each questionnaire sheet.

Controls for Threats to Validity

Several steps were taken to address threats to internal validity. Order effects were reduced by randomizing word assignment and session order across participants. Practice effects were mitigated by randomizing word assignment and order, and by administering the daily Wordle as a mandatory warm-up so that any improvement from familiarity with the interface would manifest before study tasks began. Regression to the mean was addressed by including the warm-up Wordle to bring participants closer to their average performance before data collection commenced. Beginner’s luck was similarly reduced by the warm-up task.

Data Analysis

Two operational definitions were applied before analysis to ensure data quality. OD1 (guidance compliance) retained only participants who used LOOIE as their first guess in both W3 and W4, reducing the sample from n = 23 to n = 20 (P2, P11, and P19 excluded). OD2 (correct answer scoring) coded a Wordle as solved only if the participant’s final guess exactly matched the secret word, overriding any self-reported solve status contradicted by the raw guess record. The primary statistical analysis was a series of one-sided paired-samples t-tests comparing turn-rate between non-guided and guided sessions. Effect sizes were computed using Cohen’s d. Secondary analyses included point-biserial correlations and independent-samples t-tests for questionnaire data. Performance was also compared against New York Times WordleBot global averages from a population of approximately 400,000 players.

Pilot Testing

A pilot test was conducted to verify the methods that were to be employed in this experiment. Researchers developed focus groups of 2 participants each for the purposes of verifying the likely success of the proposed methodology. A total of 8 participants participated in the pilot test with ages ranging from the 20s to 60s. Older participants took more time to complete the Wordles they attempted and also discontinued play before attempting strategically guided Wordles. The older participant time data from pilot testing was disregarded for the experiment due to the proposed experiment sample being of a much younger demographic; the younger pilot testing participants successfully completed the experiment in the final allotted time. The desertion rate of older participants attributed to frustration and stress was also disregarded due to the differences in age; it was assumed that differences in fluid and crystallized intelligence in the experiment sample compared to the pilot group were responsible for this.

Additionally, participants that were similar in age and background to the sample group reported repetitiveness in the provided questionnaire; this feedback was taken into consideration and resulted in the questions being changed to reduce redundancy and increase participant engagement. A variety of participants reported a lack of usefulness regarding the guidance words, specifically LOOIE; they did not find strategic guidance helpful or compatible with the task at hand. This opinion was carefully considered, but did not lead to the methodology being changed due to increased efficiency and accuracy for word guesses observed during pilot testing. In a similar vein, participants reported a lack of enjoyment while playing Wordle with strategic guidance and stated that the guidance took the fun out of the game. Although these opinions were valid, they were ultimately considered inconsequential to the outcome of this study. This experiment was focused primarily on the efficiency and accuracy of participants; participants, despite their repeated complaints, were solving more efficiently and accurately, which suggested that the proposed methodology for the experiment would prove successful.

Results

01

Statistical Analysis

The primary statistical analysis used in this study was a series of one-sided paired-samples t-tests. Because this was a within-groups design — all participants completed both the non-guided (control) and guided (experimental) conditions — paired t-tests were appropriate for directly comparing each participant's performance across conditions while controlling for individual differences. A one-sided test was specified because our hypothesis was directional: we predicted that the guided condition would produce more turns (higher turn-rate) than the non-guided condition, as the prescribed strategy sequence (LOOIE → SAURY → CHANT → BIDED → PRIMP) was hypothesized to restructure the problem-solving process rather than shortcut it. Effect sizes were calculated using Cohen's d to assess practical significance alongside statistical significance.

Methodology & Hypothesis

This study employed a quasi-experimental within-groups design. Twenty-three psychology students at Kwantlen Polytechnic University (PSYC 2400, Langley campus) completed four Wordle puzzles in sequence: two in the non-guided condition (W1 and W2, free strategy) followed by two in the guided condition (W3 and W4, prescribed starter sequence). It is considered quasi-experimental because participants were not randomly assigned to conditions; rather, all participants completed both conditions in a fixed order by design. The independent variable was strategic guidance condition; the dependent variable was turn-rate and solve success. To contextualize performance, study results were compared against New York Times WordleBot global averages drawn from a population of approximately 400,000 players in the most recent available year. The hypothesis was: if logical, strategic guidance is applied, Wordle turn-rate will increase relative to the non-guided condition. To reduce practice effects and beginners’ luck, all participants completed the New York Times daily Wordle on March 11th, 2026 — that day’s word being TEDDY — as a mandatory warm-up before beginning any study tasks. Word assignments across the four study sessions were also randomized across participants to reduce order effects.

From Raw Data to Analyzable Data

Raw data were collected on paper questionnaire booklets and transcribed into a master spreadsheet. Two operational definitions were applied before analysis. OD1 (guidance compliance) retained only participants who used LOOIE as their first guess in both W3 and W4, reducing the sample from n = 23 to n = 20. Three participants were excluded: P2 opened W4 with YOLUS, P11 reversed the sequence order, and P19’s notes explicitly stated non-compliance. OD2 (correct answer scoring) coded a Wordle as solved only if the participant’s final guess exactly matched the secret word — overriding any self-reported solve status contradicted by the raw record. Seven corrections were made in total; the most consequential was P17, who self-reported solving both W3 and W4 but whose final guesses (GOALS, CRAMP) did not match the target words (RATTY, HABIT).

To establish whether participants were already using strategic approaches before receiving guidance, three strategy questions were included in the W2 questionnaire. Discussion

The present study examined whether strategic guidance derived from Anderson and Meyer’s (2022) reinforcement learning model would increase Wordle turn rates among university students while improving solve success. Across most performance metrics, guidance significantly increased turn rate in the first guided session but did not produce consistent improvements in efficiency across both guided sessions. A consistent downward trend in completion time was observed across W1–W4 (5.16 → 4.45 → 4.17 → 3.53 min; see Figures 2–3), though no individual paired t-test reached significance. These results are not without meaning. Taken together with the questionnaire data, they surface a set of unanswered questions and important qualifications to the existing literature examined in the introduction.

Anderson and Meyer (2022): From Computers to Humans

Anderson and Meyer’s strategy was generated across 10,000 simulated trials, producing an optimal, computationally derived heuristic. The three-step rule — start with high-information words, exclude on yellow, solve on green — is theoretically elegant, but our results suggest that elegance in simulation does not straightforwardly transfer to performance in humans under real-time conditions. Guidance compliance significantly predicted whether participants solved the puzzle at all (rpb = .436, p = .005; see Figure 15), indicating the strategy was not inert. It helped participants complete the puzzle: solve rate improved by 12.5 percentage points (50.0% → 62.5%), though guided sessions averaged 0.58 more turns overall (4.83 non-guided vs. 5.40 guided; see Figure 1), meaning the strategy traded efficiency for completion.

One plausible explanation concerns the nature of the prescribed starter words themselves. Words like LOOIE and SAURY are statistically powerful within Anderson and Meyer’s reinforcement learning framework but are lexically unusual in everyday English. Participants familiar with common vocabulary may have found these words cognitively disruptive rather than orienting — an intuition consistent with anecdotal feedback from P19 and a pilot-test participant, where LOOIE was described as counterproductive precisely because of its double-O overlap. Additionally, several of Anderson and Meyer’s highest-entropy openers (e.g., BOWNE, SOOEY, SALET) are not accepted by the official New York Times’ Wordle word validator, meaning the list deployed in practice may have been suboptimal even by the standard of their own model. Anderson and Meyer themselves acknowledge that humans cannot perform the complex calculations their agent performs; our data extend that acknowledgment by suggesting that even when humans are told the output of those calculations, translating a prescribed list into a dynamic, adaptive game strategy remains cognitively demanding.

The guidance may therefore have introduced a secondary cognitive load — managing the rules themselves — that partially offset any entropy-related benefit. This connects directly to Loibl and Rummel’s (2014) framework, addressed further below.

Achaa-Amankwaa et al. (2025): Natural Strategy and Its Limits

The questionnaire data from Wordle 2 (unguided) reveal that participants already exhibited behaviours broadly consistent with Achaa-Amankwaa and colleagues’ entropy framework. Colour feedback from previous guesses was the dominant natural strategy (M = 3.95, SD = 1.05; 80% agreement; see Figures 10–11), while starting with vowel-rich words and minimizing letter overlap were more evenly split near neutral (40% and 30% agreement respectively; see Figure 11). Without any instruction, university-level participants were already partially applying information-efficient strategies — the very behaviours Achaa-Amankwaa et al. associate with higher Wordle proficiency.

The puzzle is why participants who reported minimizing overlap did not perform notably better. In fact, the positive trend for overlap-minimization (see Figure 10) suggests that those who claimed to avoid overlap may have paradoxically used more guesses, possibly by over-eliminating. Why, then, did imposing a formal entropy-maximizing strategy through guidance not produce the expected gains? Part of the answer may lie in cultural and linguistic context. Achaa-Amankwaa et al.’s study is grounded entirely in a German-language Wordle, with German entropy examples and a German-speaking adult pilot sample. The distribution of common letters, vowel positions, and consonant cluster patterns differs substantially between German and English. Our participants were predominantly English-speaking Canadian university students playing a New York Times English Wordle with words like MUGGY, PITCH, and RATTY — words that reflect English phonotactic constraints. Whether entropy-based thinking as a cognitive behaviour is scaffolded by one’s native linguistic intuitions about a specific language remains an open empirical question.

Achaa-Amankwaa et al. predict that Wordle proficiency loads primarily on crystallized intelligence (Gc) and retrieval ability (Gr), with a moderate contribution from fluid intelligence (Gf). A useful way to conceptualize this: Gc is the library — the larger it is, the more words and linguistic rules a player has available to draw on. Gr is the search engine — how quickly information is retrieved. Gf is processing power — capacity to run complex conditional logic in real time. Our participant pool likely brought moderate Gc in English but limited strategic Gf scaffolding. Guidance was designed to compensate by providing Gf-equivalent output externally. That this intervention failed to produce consistent turn-rate reductions suggests the interface between an external rule and a player’s crystallized linguistic knowledge is more fraught than anticipated. Participants who reported complying with guidance were significantly more likely to solve the puzzle (rpb = .436, p = .005; see Figure 15), but high self-reported compliance (M = 3.55, 70% fully followed; see Figure 12) did not translate into fewer guesses. Compliance as reported and compliance as enacted may have diverged: participants followed the starter words but were unable to execute the branching logic adaptively in the game. Gf played a role — but in how well participants could interpret the rule, not simply in whether they tried to follow it.

Loibl and Rummel (2014): The “Problem-Solving Space” Problem

Loibl and Rummel found that guidance through contrast cases narrowed the problem-solving space and improved the immediate quality of students’ solutions — yet this did not translate into better posttest performance than the unguided condition. In the present study, the guided condition produced significantly more turns than the unguided condition (W2 vs. W3: p = .003, d = −0.69; see Figure 6), meaning the basic precondition of Loibl and Rummel’s framework — that guidance improves immediate performance — was not met. Furthermore, rather than improving immediate performance, our guidance appeared to interfere with it, at least initially.

Our study was premised on Anderson and Meyer’s (2022) mechanism: start with high-entropy words (LOOIE → SAURY → CHANT → BIDED → PRIMP) in sequence, with a conditional decision tree intended to dramatically constrain participants’ search space. However, examining Appendix D reveals an intervention considerably more complex than a simple constraint. Participants received a five-word ordered list, a three-step conditional rule with branching logic, a table of edge cases, and colour-coded instructions, all on top of a verbal explanation delivered mid-experiment. Rather than narrowing the problem-solving space, this effectively widened it by adding a parallel task: reading, remembering, and applying a novel procedural rule while simultaneously playing a fast-paced word game.

This connects back to Achaa-Amankwaa et al.’s (2025) framework: guidance was premised on the assumption that participants would lack sufficient Gf to play optimally. But by delivering that scaffold as a dense written sheet mid-session, we may have demanded the very Gf capacity we were trying to replace. Loibl and Rummel’s guidance worked through contrast cases — a format that highlights one key structural difference at a time. Ours presented multiple decision points simultaneously, which may have produced something closer to cognitive overload than productive constraint.

The fact that perceived usefulness was only 2.95 out of 5 in Wordle 3 (40% rating it helpful or very helpful) and improved to 3.40 in Wordle 4 (60% helpful; see Figures 13–14) supports this interpretation: participants were still processing the guidance during Wordle 3 and only began to internalize it by Wordle 4.

Dixon et al. (2024): Participant 19 and the Goal-Gradient Dilemma

Dixon and colleagues demonstrate that Wordle is not merely a cognitive task but an emotionally driven one, where approach and thwarted outcomes produce measurable differences in motivation, positive affect, and frustration. Our study indirectly observed this dynamic between Wordle 3 and Wordle 4. Of note, P19 — excluded under OD1 — left an explicit written note on their raw data sheet: “First guess being one with a double ‘O’ seems unhelpful.” This mirrors the experience of a pilot-test participant who shared the same anecdote and similarly rejected the prescribed starting word, directly illustrating purposeful non-compliance driven by intuitive resistance to the guidance.

This finding ties directly onto Dixon et al.’s goal-gradient framework. The “if only yellows, EXCLUDE” step (Appendix D) requires players to deliberately ignore yellow tiles they have just guessed until a green tile appears. From a goal-gradient perspective, this is quite counterintuitive: participants are being asked to act as though they are moving away from their goal at the very moment they can see they are approaching it. Dixon et al. show that approach outcomes — new coloured tiles — trigger significantly higher positive affect and motivation, while thwarted outcomes produce frustration and motivational drops. Our strategic guidance prescribed precisely that: a sequence of deliberate thwart-like moves as the core mechanism, instructing participants to continue down the five-word list until a green letter registers, even when yellow and repeat letters are being revealed. This is especially evident with OMEGA, for which the full prescribed list — LOOIE → SAURY → CHANT → BIDED → PRIMP — coincidentally yielded all yellow and gray letters.

Taken together with the Loibl and Rummel (2014) analysis and P19’s written refusal, the guidance was not only cognitively demanding — it was also emotionally aversive. This is consistent with the perceived usefulness rating in Wordle 3 (M = 2.95; see Figure 13), which is below the neutral midpoint.

Limitations

This study has potential limits that stem from the methodology of the experiment. First, the methodological choice of using convenience sampling resulted in a smaller sample size than initially predicted. The sample size was further reduced due to participant availability on the day of the experiment and due to OD1 exclusions. Furthermore, the data collected from participants was entirely self-report, and some discrepancies existed in the answers provided. Participants may have felt overwhelmed due to the high cognitive load required during the experiment, resulting in an inability to correctly comprehend and answer the questions.

On February 2nd, 2026, the New York Times announced that it would be resetting the word bank available for Wordle in an attempt to extend the lifetime of the game (McLaren, 2023). This resulted in limiting the available target words to only those from before the reset date, to ensure that archival data remained available and unchanged. Additionally, due to the inclusion of data only available through WordleBot, the words provided as strategic guidance were altered for use in the New York Times’ Create Your Own Word puzzle format. According to the New York Times, there are almost 15,000 five-letter words in the Wordle dictionary that a player is allowed to guess (New York Times Wordle Bot F.A.Q., n.d.) when playing the official game. However, WordleBot only allows approximately 4,500 words, requiring words to be considerably common before being permitted. The original words selected for strategic guidance — including BOWNE, SOOEY, and SALET — were reconsidered and swapped because they could not be processed by WordleBot despite their acceptance in the official game.

It is noteworthy that some participants commented on the incompatibility of the guidance words with the target words. Specifically, P19 stated that “the first guess being one with a double ‘O’ seems unhelpful” when prompted to use LOOIE as their first guess. Interestingly, this participant was able to successfully solve the target word faster than their unguided attempts despite this objection. Moreover, a pilot-test participant shared this perspective while also completing Wordles faster after the inclusion of strategic guidance. Because of the contradictions between the Wordle game and WordleBot word banks, participants’ concerns about the starting word LOOIE in particular were incorrectly deemed inconsequential to the outcome of the study.

A concern with the issue of bombardment was prevalent throughout the proposal and conduction of the study. Participants were provided numerous sheets of paper consisting of instructions and a question and answer booklet, resulting in a relatively high degree of reading. Pilot testing raised minimal concern for bombardment, which resulted in testing procedures staying unchanged, but the high word count in tandem with other procedural restrictions may have impacted how participants performed. A verbal accompaniment to the written materials was provided to all participants, but this relied heavily on participant willingness to ask for clarification.

Other potential limitations include the rate of multilingual verbal fluency and time constraints among the available sample. As participants’ linguistic fluency was not considered relevant for testing purposes, the data does not differentiate between multilingual and monolingual participants. Previous research partially supports that “language-specific learning could account for performance, [while] formal [second language] training would predict skills” (Blumenfeld et al., 2021). Additionally, the original Wordle word list was over 12,000 words (Victor, 2022), but was reduced to approximately 2,500 depending on the obscurity and commonality of words in English. Despite attempts to make the game more accessible for those with a limited English lexicon, many words appear that are not commonly used by either native English speakers or those who have learned English as a supplemental language.

This study was also required to adhere to a time limit. Despite permission to continue past the originally stipulated duration, participants may have felt time pressure that resulted in altered performance. The setting of a university classroom, combined with the student background of the participants, may have added undue stress. Pre-testing procedures showed variable range in time taken, yet the experimental design did not change to add more time past the 40-minute limit.

Minor methodological revisions could address several of these limitations in future replications. With the removal of the custom Wordle tool by the New York Times, participants could use the originally selected guidance words adapted from Anderson and Meyer (2022), eliminating complaints regarding the usefulness of the guidance words and the WordleBot word bank constraint. The issue of bombardment, undue stress, and the time limit could be reduced through the restructuring of experimental materials and instruction delivery. An interactive visual representation for both how to play Wordle and how to employ the strategic guidance — replacing Appendices B and D — would reduce reading demands and provide more time for gameplay. Ideally, an alternative Wordle in the primary language of multilingual participants would have been made available; in lieu of that, an additional self-report question would help control for confounds associated with second language skills and performance.

Further research on this topic is essential, not only because there is limited empirical data surrounding Wordle, but because some questions still remain unanswered. A study currently under review by Achaa-Amankwaa et al. (2025) attempts to observe whether Wordle functions as a reliable measure of verbal ability and seeks answers to whether a primary form of intelligence or retrieval ability drives performance. A key aspect that came to light upon reviewing raw data was the intensity of emotions during gameplay. Emotions, although important in the context of Wordle through the use of coloured feedback and goal gradients (Dixon et al., 2024), were not the primary focus of this study. A future study with a revised questionnaire targeting participant emotions and internal mechanisms rather than perceived performance would add meaningfully to the existing literature.

Conclusion

The goal of this study was to design a preliminary test on the potential of Wordle as a structural puzzle measuring fluid reasoning ability. It was hypothesized that strategic guidance would increase Wordle turn-rate while improving solve success, suggesting that Wordle is more similar to cryptic crosswords than simple crosswords in that it was more likely to require fluid reasoning rather than crystallized intelligence. Taking inspiration from studies conducted by Loibl and Rummel (2014) and Achaa-Amankwaa et al. (2025), this experiment used a high probability word list by Anderson and Meyer (2022) to test the effects of strategic guidance on performance by assessing verbal ability. A conveniently sampled group of university students were instructed to perform two unguided Wordles before being provided with written and verbal instructions for strategic guidance words that were used to help with the remaining two Wordles. The data was collected through the use of self-report surveys and was subsequently analysed following data restriction using the following operational definitions: 1) the participant correctly used strategic guidance; and 2) the participant’s final guess matched the target word.

Ultimately, strategic guidance significantly increased turn rate in the first guided session (W2 vs. W3: p = .003, d = −0.69) and compliance with the prescribed strategy significantly predicted solve success (rpb = .436, p = .005). However, guidance did not significantly reduce completion time, and the effect on turn rate did not persist into the second guided session (W2 vs. W4: p = .075). While guidance did increase turn rate as predicted, it did not improve overall efficiency, suggesting that the cognitive demands of applying the prescribed strategy may have offset its practical benefits. Many factors, including but not limited to sampling error, time constraints, a bombardment of information, and structural limitations, may have served as confounds for this study. Promising ongoing research studies awaiting publication may fill gaps in the current literature while future studies may serve to address the unanswered questions that arose from this study; specifically, whether Wordle functions as a reliable measure of verbal ability and is primarily a form of fluid intelligence, crystallized intelligence, or retrieval ability. The key takeaway from this study is that guidance negatively impacted efficiency while positively impacting accuracy. Although the results are not uniformly statistically significant across all comparisons, they serve to further discussion regarding whether the sacrifice of efficiency is worth the increase in accuracy.
