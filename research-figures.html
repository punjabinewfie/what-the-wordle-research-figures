The full prescribed sequence (LOOIE → SAURY → CHANT → BIDED → PRIMP) applied to OMEGA. Every guess yields only yellow and gray tiles — no green letter ever registers across all five starter words, requiring participants to override the goal-gradient instinct for all five turns before solving on the sixth.

From a goal-gradient perspective, this is maximally counterintuitive: participants are told to act as though moving away from their goal at the very moment yellow tiles signal proximity to it. This illustrates the core tension in Dixon et al. (2024): the strategy demands deliberate thwart-like behaviour as its central mechanism.

OMEGA was one of six target words randomly drawn from the 300 New York Times daily Wordles released before the cutoff date. Its alignment with the prescribed sequence was incidental, not selected.

Prescribed sequence applied:

LOOIE → SAURY → CHANT → BIDED → PRIMP

Result for OMEGA
All guided participants reached 6 turns (ceiling). Mean guided turns = 6.00 vs. non-guided mean = 5.17. This word alone accounts for a significant portion of the guided condition’s elevated turn rate.

P19 (excluded under OD1) left an explicit written note: “First guess being one with a double ‘O’ seems unhelpful.” A pilot-test participant independently shared the same objection. Both rejected LOOIE despite the guidance, directly illustrating purposeful non-compliance driven by intuitive resistance. Dixon et al.’s goal-gradient framework predicts exactly this: prescribing deliberate exclusion of visually proximate yellow information conflicts with the emotional approach-motivation that drives natural Wordle strategy.
