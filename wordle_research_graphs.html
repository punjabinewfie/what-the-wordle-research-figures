Cohen's d effect sizes with 95% CI

Paired turn comparisons  ·  n = 20 pairs

Positive d Negative d

W2–W3 yielded the largest effect (d = −0.69, medium), indicating notably more turns in the first guided session than the second unguided. W1–W2 is the only positive d (0.34), suggesting marginal improvement from W1 to W2.

Paired t-test results — mean turn difference

One-sided; hypothesis: guided > non-guided

Significant n.s.

W2–W3 was the only highly significant comparison (p = .003**), showing participants used significantly more turns in the first guided session. W1–W3 reached p = .039*. Neither W1–W2 nor W2–W4 reached significance.

Average time (min) per Wordle — solved vs. not solved

95% CI shown  ·  overall and by session

Solved Not solved

Solved participants were 1.63 min faster overall (3.71 vs. 5.34 min). The gap was most pronounced in W1 and W2 (unguided); it narrowed substantially in W3 and W4, suggesting guidance may have levelled completion times regardless of outcome.

02

Questionnaire Analysis

Perceived difficulty by Wordle session

Scale: 1 = very easy → 5 = very difficult  ·  95% CI shown

Non-guided Guided

Perceived difficulty was remarkably stable across all four sessions (range: 3.15–3.32), with wide overlapping CIs. Guided sessions (W3–W4) were not perceived as notably easier than unguided ones.

Perceived difficulty by secret word

Sorted hardest → easiest  ·  95% CI shown

MUGGY was rated hardest (4.17) and had the second-lowest solve rate (38.5%). PITCH was rated easiest (2.43) and had a 71.4% solve rate, broadly consistent with objective difficulty.

Perceived difficulty — solved vs. not solved

Independent t-test: t = −3.28, p = .002**  ·  Solved rated significantly easier

Solved (n = 45) Not solved (n = 34)

Participants who solved their Wordle rated it 0.72 points easier on average (2.93 vs. 3.65), a significant difference: t(77) = −3.28, p = .002**. This suggests perceived difficulty tracks actual performance.

W2 natural strategy — agreement ratings

Scale: 1 = strongly disagree → 5 = strongly agree  ·  n = 20  ·  95% CI shown

Mean agreement

Colour feedback was the most strongly endorsed strategy (mean = 3.95; 80% agreed or strongly agreed). Neither vowel-rich starts nor minimising letter overlap significantly predicted guess count (both p > .10).

W2 strategy — full response distributions

Count per rating level (1–5) for each strategy question

1 – Strongly disagree 2 – Disagree 3 – Neutral 4 – Agree 5 – Strongly agree

Colour feedback shows strong right-skew (16 of 20 agreed/strongly agreed). Letter overlap minimisation had the most disagreement (7 rated 2). Vowel-rich start responses were evenly spread across all five ratings.

Perceived usefulness of guidance

Scale: 1 = very unhelpful → 5 = very helpful  ·  95% CI shown

Guided sessions

Usefulness increased W3 → W4 (2.95 → 3.40); % rating 4+ rose from 40% to 60%

Usefulness rating distributions — W3 vs. W4

Count per rating level (1–5)

1 – Very unhelpful 2 – Unhelpful 3 – Neutral 4 – Helpful 5 – Very helpful

W4 shows a clear shift toward helpful ratings: the proportion rating 4 or 5 rose from 40% (W3) to 60% (W4), while ratings of 1 dropped from 4 to 3. Participants appeared to find guidance more useful with repeated exposure.

Correlations — guidance & usefulness vs. outcomes

W3+W4 pooled  ·  n = 40  ·  r / rpb values shown

Positive r Near zero / n.s.

Guidance compliance was the only significant predictor: higher compliance strongly predicted solving (rpb = .44, p = .005**). Perceived usefulness showed only a non-significant trend toward fewer guesses (r = −.28, p = .079). These findings suggest that following the strategy matters more than believing in it.
